A Comparison of the Properties of Selected Commercially Available, Low-cost CO2 and CH4 Gas Concentration Sensors

Wesley T. Honeycutt, Nicholas F. Materer, Tyler M. Ley

Abstract This paper is a comparison of several commercially available, low-cost CO2 and CH4 gas concentration sensors. Sensors which were reported to be able measure gas concentration of the selected molecules below 1000 ppm were selected based on market availability and reported capabilities by the manufacturer. The measurement capabilities of the sensors were determined using a custom-built gas mixing chamber and a high-end gas analysis device to act as the standard. The accuracy, precision, and detection limit were determined for each sensor and compared. Where applicable, the rise time of the sensor response was measured and recorded.

Index Terms Chemical sensors, Gas industry, Pollution measurement, Spectroscopy, Carbon, Chemistry, Gas detectors, Sensor systems and applications

I. Introduction

DEVICES dependent on accurate gas concentration analysis require the best possible sensors within the confines of the project. These small, inexpensive sensors are used for a range of devices from HVAC air handlers  [1, 2], to chemical processing units  [3], to oil well monitoring devices  [4, 5], to envinronmental monitoring  [6, 7, 8, 9, 10], and to a myriad of other applications. The work cited here is only a sample of the various applications, and there are many other extant examples. Little work has been published comparing these types of sensors to each other to determine which is best for such applications. This paper will compare an array of commercially available, low-cost sensors for CO2 and CH4 concentration detection which might be used in such projects.

II. Objectives

The objective of this research was to determine the capabilities of some of these commercially available low-cost sensors which are designed for simple integration into usable devices. The sensors tested in this work either output responses via a known protocol such as an RS-232 serial communications protocol or via simple voltage changes in the sensing unit. Those with a known protocol are easiest to integrate with electronics. The response can be interpreted simply by a computer or microcontroller unit. The devices which only output voltage require additional electronics to be usable, such as a level converter, to provide meaningful output to the user. For the purposes of our tests, sensors were monitored by an Atmel AVR microcontroller by way of an Arduino prototyping board. The use of the Arduino board simplified the connections and allowed the tests to be conducted in a timely manner, without requiring the development of a control unit.

III. Methods

Several commercially available CO2 and CH4 sensors were selected and evaluated. Table I lists the tested CO2 sensors with important properties listed by the manufacturer, and Table II lists the CH4 sensors and respective properties. Certain sensor units were chosen for specific promised abilities. Notably, the K-30, COZIR, Dynament, and Telaire sensors were chosen and cheap, lightweight sensors which promised satisfactory detection of CO2 . Dynament also offered a dual gas sensor which was designed to measure both CO2 and CH4 concentrations simultaneously. The Gascard sensors sold by GHG Analytical were an order of magnitude more costly than the other sensors, but the advertised capability made them attractive enough to make up for the expense. The MQ-4 and Dynament hydrocarbon sensors were chosen as the inexpensive candidates for CH4 detection. Due to the unique interface requirements of each sensor unit, development kits were purchased when possible.

To test the sensors, an experimental setup (Fig. 1) was built. The aim was to allow the calibration process for the gas analyzer to be continually preformed. A tank of mixed gases containing either 3000 ppm CO2 (±3%), 3000 ppm CH4 (±3%), and N2 certified by Airgas Inc. or 100 ppm CO2 (±3%), 100 ppm CH4 (±3%), and N2 certified by Airgas Inc., depending on the specific experiment being performed, was mixed with bulk N2 gas by flow control to produce specific partial pressures of gases relevant to the test. The sensors were connected to the apparatus by flexible hose connection for sensors requiring controlled gas flow in and out through ports on the sensor or tested in the exposure chamber in the case of sensors which work by diffusion with a local atmosphere. Fig. 2 shows the exposure apparatus. Flow mode through the gas flow chamber allows investigation of mass transfer effects through sensors which incorporate a membrane. The time constant for the gas to disperse within the chamber was determined to be negligible with respect to the response of the instruments. All experiments were referenced by a high-quality bench-top analyzer (California Analytical Instruments, Inc. ZRE Non-Dispersive Infrared Analyzer) sensitive to both CO2 and CH4 . Sensors collected data and were powered by an Arduino microcontroller connected to a computer by USB cable in cases where a development board for the individual sensor could not be obtained.

IV. Results and Discussion

The tests showed that the Edinburgh Gascard sensors most reliably gave accurate information about the gas concentration. The GE Telaire and CO2Meter K-30 sensor both gave reasonable results for sensors in their price range, though the Telaire sensor did exhibit issues related interference from ambient visible light. The Dynament sensors and CO2Meter COZIR sensor were found to be less reliable than other sensors in their cohort. Finally, the MQ-4 sensor proved to be rather inaccurate for gauging gas concentration, rather it is more suitable to giving a rough idea of the presence of CH4.

A. Accuracy and Precision Tests

The precision and accuracy of the measurement was performed using the exposure apparatus discussed above. The CO2 tests were performed with a mixed gas concentration of approximately 400 ppm in N2, while the CH4 tests were performed with a mixed gas concentration of 0 ppm. The null CH4 concentration was chosen due to the MQ-4 sensor. The MQ-4 sensor requires O2 to be present to measure the gas, and to allow for comparable results between the CH4 sensors, testing under pure N2 was deemed the most appropriate option. Early tests attempted to test the CH4 sensors under mixed gas in purified air, but it was suspected that this might interfere with the Dynament dual-gas sensor in the presence of CO2 . While there is low probability there would be interference, efforts were taken to ensure to isolate possible interference rather than risk producing poor data. The concentration of gas in the chamber was verified with the high-powered ZRE FTIR. The precision of the sensors was determined by a 20-30 hour data collection time at a known concentration and uniform flow. For all sensors, first a Fourier analysis was performed. The Fourier analysis showed no distinct periodic variations in the output during this test. Next, the data streams were processed to provide the individual difference from the mean and a histogram of these differences was plotted. The sensor outputs have a finite number of granular possible output values, so no further bins were created while producing the analysis. A Gaussian fit was applied to the histograms to determine the degree of fit. The histograms with Gaussian peak fitting can be seen in Figs. 3 and 4 for the CO2 and CH4 sensors, respectively. Finally, the drift was measured using the difference between the highest and lowest points in an extended run. The summary of results from this analysis can be found in Tables III and IV.

The Gascard and K-30 SE-0018 sensors both showed relatively narrow distributions of response. The Gascard shows a strong degree of fit with the Gaussian plot, giving a full width at half max (FWHM) of 5.01 ppm. The K-30 SE-0018 tends toward higher concentration values and appears to show two overlapping peaks. Possible periodic temperature and pressure variations, which are corrected for by the Gascard sensor, are likely responsible. It is suggested that implementation of the K-30 sensor also incorporate pressure and temperature sensing units to correct these deviations in post-processing. The breadth of the strongest Gaussian peak in the histogram of responses from the K-30 sensor suggests a similarly narrow distribution around the mean as the Gascard. For both of these sensors, the drift was ±20 ppm. The distribution of responses around the mean by the GE Telaire sensor produces a more squat peak than that of the K-30 and GasCard. The broadening of this peak (FWHM 10.4) is interesting, as the mechanism and path length in the Telaire is very similar to that employed by the K-30 sensor. It was hypothesized that light might be interacting with the sensors. A simple test was performed on both the Telaire and K-30 sensor whereby the sensor response to atmospheric CO2 was monitored in darkness, then light was applied. This test showed that the Telaire sensor is significantly more sensitive to outside light sources than the K-30. The change in response from the K-30 sensor during this test was negligible, while the data reported by the Telaire was observed to shift noticeably. It is probable that the peak broadening for the Telaire sensor was due in part to this effect. The COZIR AMB GC-020 sensor produced a wide range of responses, and can be considered to be less precise than the K-30 sensor, which is produced by the same manufacturer. The Dynament sensors were shown to be very imprecise. Of note is the difference in the FWHM of reported values between the two Dynament sensors tested. While the single-gas sensor gave a level of precision on par with the COZIR sensor, the dual-gas sensor reported CO2 concentrations over a wide span of values.

Of the CH4 sensors tested, the Edinburgh Gascard again proved the most precise. The normal distribution of responses around the reported mean from the Gascard is a very sharp peak. The Gascard did report some relatively extreme values, several peak widths higher than the mean response. This error cannot be explained, and may be specific to the unit in question. The single gas Dynament sensor produced a narrow distribution of responses, but the results from the dual-gas Dynament sensor were inconclusive. This sensor only reported two values, rather than a normal distribution of several values. As the FWHM could not be reported for this, we must assume that the distribution of responses is the difference between these two points around a mean. With this reasoning, the precision of the dual-gas sensor is very nearly equivalent to the FWHM of the responses from the single-gas sensor. The MQ-4 sensor produced a wide range of values. The spread of responses from this sensor is so broad, that it is out of scale in Figs. 4. While the FWHM of these responses from the MQ-4 show that the peak only deviates 1.19 ppm around the mean, it is comparatively much less precise than the other sensors in the experiment.

With the exception of the CO2 and CH4 Gascard sensors, there was poor accuracy. In the case of some sensors including the K-30, Telaire, COZIR, and Dynament CO2 sensors, this issue could be rectified by recalibration of the sensors. All of these sensors must be calibrated before use and cannot be used directly from the supplier if the user hopes to obtain an accurate concentration value. For some of the tested devices such as the MQ-4 CH4 sensor, good accuracy may be outside of the reasonable expectation of sensor ability.

B. Response Time

Most of the sensors tested began to register change in concentration on a timescale which could not be measured accurately within the scope of this project. This was verified by determining the amount of time for the gas to travel the plumbing to the ZRE detector, registering a response. With this information, a flow rate was calculated, and applied to the apparatus. A back-of-the-envelope estimation was made with this value to confirm that the sensors were responding in a reasonable time, which will not be discussed here. For our purposes, the response was immediate.

The sensor which responded on a notably different timescale was the MQ-4 sensor. It was observed that a change in the response from the sensor was first observed immediately, for practical purposes, to the gas being introduced to the system. However, this value did not immediately represent the concentration of gas in the system. A test was conducted to determine the response time of this sensor by introduction of gas to the system until a stable value was produced, then vented with purified air until the previous baseline was regained. The ratio of gas introduced to the system was changed between each acquisition period to determine if there was a relationship between the concentration of introduced gas and the time to produce a stable response. A smaller sensor enclosure (internal volume 1 in3) was constructed to minimize error from gas mixing in a large chamber, Fig. 5 depicts a plot of the responses from one of these experiments. The sensor produces a sharp overshoot, with some instances displaying a percentage overshoot exceeding 100% of the final concentration determined by the system. Minor signal ringing follows this spike, until the response is made stable. A smaller overshoot can be observed when the mix gas is shut off, and replaced by pure air. The MQ-4 works by a thermometric response, and as such, it is reasonable that the mixed gas introduced to the system changed the temperature near the sensor, and this contributed to the degree of overshoot observed. This would not explain the overshoot when the gas was switched to pure air, as it was the same temperature as the mixed gas.

Fig. 5.  The MQ-4 sensor showed noticeable delay between introduction of gas and production of a stable response.

The rise time of a system, defined as the time for the response to change from 10% of the mean response to 90% of the mean response, is less useful for this system due to the extreme overshoot observed. Settling time, the time for a system to change from the baseline to a window around the mean during the ringing portion, is more useful for this application. The settling time for the MQ-4 sensor did not appear to be concentration dependent. A stable value was produced within 2.5% of the mean after 78±10 s, averaged over all response time experiments.

C. Limit of Detection

Utilizing the gas mixing apparatus discussed in Section III, a series of tests were conducted to determine the limit of detection and response curve of the selected sensors. For these tests, multiple sensors were in operation concurrently as shown in Figure III. The sensors are organized by flow-based sensors such as the Gascard units and exposure-based sensors housed within the gas enclosures. Sensor modules in the small gas flow enclosures contain a K30 CO2 sensor and a socket for thermo-reactive CH4 sensors such as the MQ4 and those in the TGS series. The large gas flow chamber contained, for these experiments, the COZIR, Telaire, Dynament, and K30 sensors. The order of instruments in the gas flow stream was found to be important, as the Gascard sensors require an adequate back pressure to operate. Placing these sensors near the gas inlet prevents leaks forming from the flow chambers.
